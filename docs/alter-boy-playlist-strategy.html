The deep-dive on the first leg of The Plan — Discover. How we get onto the algorithmic playlists on our own terms, generate the signals that make the algorithm carry us, and turn cold listeners into the start of a path that ends in a room. We borrow the Intellijend release engine's mechanics; we never borrow its faceless, single-mill creative.

01 — Where we are

Five years in, the old doors are getting harder to push open.

We're not the new band anymore. The pathways that used to open for new acts — high-rotation triple j, the "freshest find" editorial spots, that first-spin attention — increasingly don't open as readily for acts with a catalogue behind them. What is still wide open, and genuinely doesn't care how long we've been going, is the algorithmic layer: Discover Weekly and Release Radar on Spotify, New Music Mix and Favourites Mix on Apple, the equivalents on Tidal and YouTube Music.

There's no editor to win over here. These playlists put songs in front of listeners based purely on how listeners behave — what they save, what they replay, what they skip, what they add to their own playlists. That's the opportunity: we don't need anyone's permission. It's also the bar: we have to actually generate those signals. Spotify gets most of the airtime in here because it's where most of the audience and most of the data live — but the same principles travel across the platforms.

Where this sits. The Plan lays out three moves — Discover, then the back-catalogue, then Deepen. This document is the deep-dive on the first one. Hold onto one thing the whole way through: the algorithm is never the destination. Saves and placements are how we find the people; the room is what we're finding them for. Everything below is in service of that.
02 — Where the edge is

Intellijend's engine is good. It's also built for the opposite of us — which turns out to be the edge.

Intellijend's setup automates the Meta-ads-to-Spotify pipeline: front-load the budget early, run a bunch of creative variants, drop the ones not working fast, optimise for cost-per-listener, send everything through one smart link. It works. But it works best for a very specific kind of artist — someone putting out interchangeable down-the-middle dance tracks, one a month, forever. Even their own ad packs are stock footage. The audience that liked track one likes track two because track two sounds like track one.

We're the opposite of that. And the two things their tool can't hand an artist — a recognisable world and an audience that already cares — we already have.

The point: our identity isn't a problem to work around — it's the actual edge that makes our ads stop the thumb and our saves stick. We borrow Jend's mechanics; we never borrow his creative model.

The mechanics we take wholesale

None of the following is where we're different, so we borrow it as-is and don't burn brain on it:

  • Pre-save campaign submitted to Spotify for Artists about four weeks ahead, so we land in followers' Release Radar on day one.
  • One conversion-optimised smart link as the single destination for everything — pre-save first, then save/follow once we're live.
  • Front-loaded budget concentrated in the first 3–10 days. Momentum in that window is what the algorithm actually reads.
  • Multiple creative variants running at once; drop the ones not working fast, scale up the winner.
  • Optimise for saves and cost-per-listener, not raw reach. A save is worth a lot more than a stream.
  • A consistent release rhythm so each one feeds the next and the catalogue compounds.
03 — How the machine reads us

No editor to win over — just a test we have to pass.

Every platform we care about rewards the same handful of listener behaviours. Win them and the algorithm carries the song further on its own; miss them and it quietly contains it. There's nobody to pitch — only behaviour to earn.

The behaviours that actually move us

  • Saves, Loves and library adds — the single strongest signal. A save is worth far more than a stream, so it's the thing we optimise hardest for.
  • Completion — people listening to the end rather than bailing halfway.
  • Low early skips — the first ~30 seconds have to hold attention or the algorithm reads "doesn't stick."
  • Repeat plays and re-watches — the same listener coming back, which is exactly what our layered work earns honestly (see §04).
  • Playlist adds — listeners filing us into their own rotation.

So we judge a campaign on saves and cost-per-listener, never on raw reach. One save tells the algorithm more than a thousand passive impressions — reach and impressions are diagnostics, not goals.

Day one is the exam

Why day-one matters so much. Release Radar isn't really a discovery playlist — it's a test. Spotify uses it to ask: "how do the listeners who already have some connection to this artist react to this new track?" If those first listeners skip or don't save, the algorithm reads that and quietly contains the song. Our owned audience isn't just a nice spike-generator — it's the test cohort we engineer for the algorithm to watch.

That's also why we front-load. The first 3–10 days are the window the algorithm actually reads, and concentrated momentum there is what tips it into testing the song in Discover Weekly, Radio and autoplay. Spread the same budget thin across a month and the signal never spikes hard enough to register. Momentum in that window is the whole game — the rest of this doc is about manufacturing it.

04 — Where we don't follow the template

Six places we deliberately go our own way.

1 · Creative is the moat, treated as choreography

No generic mood footage. Every ad is a bespoke cut of the live show, the Auslan, the styling, the world we already make. The way to think about Auslan in our marketing is the way Sia thought about choreography with Maddie Ziegler, or the way FKA twigs treats movement — as the song's visual signature, not as a banner about who we are. Higher craft means higher thumb-stop means higher save-rate per dollar. This is the one place we put in more effort than Jend's artists do, because it's the one place we can't be copied.

2 · One taste-world, four ways into it

Not two pools, not a "values audience." One unified taste-world — sonic neighbours plus visual-culture, movement and editorial-aesthetic audiences — split into ad sets so we can see which sub-cluster actually converts (see §05). The audience is one thing; running it in clusters is just how we read the data.

3 · Dodge the novelty ceiling on purpose

We've already named the risk ourselves — being boxed as a "queer / trans / Deaf novelty act." So the hook leads with sound and visual world; the Deaf and queer identity is texture that deepens what people feel about us once they're in, not the pitch that pulls them in. The algorithm doesn't reward admiration — it rewards repeat-plays. Goodwill that doesn't actually convert to saves quietly hurts our signal. The targeting guardrail in §05 keeps this honest on the paid side.

4 · Build the asset Jend's artists don't have — an audience we own

Five years of audience reception is sitting out there as people who know us but aren't on any list we own. Pulling them somewhere we control gives every release a coordinated day-one save spike — exactly the signal the algorithm tests on first. The backbone is an email list: it doesn't expire, can't be throttled, and survives whatever happens to any single platform. Instagram is where we already are, so it's the natural place to stay present and rally people on release day without turning into content machines. Discord we hold in reserve — a community only works if people genuinely want to talk to each other and someone keeps it alive, and a dead one is worse than none, so we stand it up only if that demand actually shows. The list carries the message; the owned surface turns day one into coordinated action rather than a passive notification, and insulates us if any single platform's algorithm or ad account ever goes sideways.

5 · Lean on the back-catalogue

Five years of releases means Spotify, Apple and YouTube already have data on us, and we have a discography for new Discover Weekly / New Music Mix listeners to fall into. New artists can't do this. New singles are the spearhead; the catalogue is the net behind them — and, as The Plan frames it, the thing that carries a curious listener across into someone who actually cares.

6 · Let the depth do the work

Our songs exist in two languages at the same time, and they don't say identical things. Auslan has its own grammar, register and emphasis — what's signed isn't a subtitle running under the song, it's a parallel expression of it. Which means there's genuinely more in our work than a single viewing can hold. The rewatch isn't a puzzle to crack — it's the natural response to art with layers, the way a film or a poem rewards a second sitting. And rewatching and re-listening are exactly the listener behaviours the algorithm pays out on. Most artists have to manufacture rewatchability. We don't have to; it's already in how we make music. Our job is just to make sure the creative shows that depth honestly, instead of flattening it into a hook.

06 — What a release looks like

One loop, repeated.

The benchmarks below are commonly-cited industry figures — treat them as targets to check against our own Spotify for Artists data, not as gospel.

T-minus 4 weeks
Prime the radar

Submit the track in Spotify for Artists and pitch it through Apple Music for Artists. Launch the pre-save through the smart link. Tease the world to the list and on Instagram — early Auslan moments, behind-the-scenes — so the audience is anticipating it (and seed fragments into Discord too, if it's running). Start warming Sub-cluster A with low-budget engagement creative.

T-minus 1 week
Load the spring

Countdown to the list and on Instagram for a coordinated day-one hit. Finalise 4–6 bespoke ad cuts, one per sub-cluster plus variants. Confirm Canvas loop, Apple Music motion artwork, pre-save numbers. Tidal Rising / Spotlight submission filed.

Days 0–3
The window

Front-load the budget hard across the four sub-clusters. Fire the list broadcast and the Instagram push on day zero — those are the test cohort the algorithm watches first. All creative variants live; we read save-rate and skip-rate, not reach.

Days 4–10
Feed the winner

Kill the ad sets that aren't working, scale the ones that are. This is the 6–10 day window where momentum decides whether the algorithm picks us up and starts testing the song in Radio / Discover Weekly / Apple's autoplay surfaces. The owned audience runs the song's depth in parallel — Instagram posts and list notes on the signed parts, the layered meanings — which drives repeat plays from our most engaged listeners right when the algorithm is watching.

Days 11–30
Catch the spillover

As the algorithmic placement kicks in, route the new listeners arriving from Discover Weekly etc. into the back-catalogue — the connector that carries them deeper. Capture them to the list, bring the most engaged closer on Instagram. Log what worked so the next cycle inherits it.

10 — Standing up and tuning the Discovery engine

Five phases. No clock.

This is the operating detail behind The Plan's five phases — the same arc, with the "what this asks" and "done when" spelled out. No fixed timeline: each phase ends on its own definition of done, and only then do we move on. Treating it as a calibration journey rather than a 90-day countdown keeps us honest — we don't burn ad spend before we have infrastructure, we don't tune before we have data, we don't lock the cadence before we've put a real release through it.

Phase 01

Foundations

Stand up the infrastructure so any release can route through one funnel and reach an audience we own on day zero.

  • Pre-save and smart-link tooling configured; one destination for all traffic.
  • Owned audience stood up: an email list (Listmonk on a small VPS, or a free email tier) as the backbone, and our Instagram sharpened into the release-day rallying surface. Discord left in reserve — sketched as a plan, stood up only if two-way demand shows.
  • Existing fanbase pulled in — Bandcamp followers, mailing-list legacy, social DMs, anyone we already informally know about — onto the list and following on Instagram.
  • Distribution metadata audit across the catalogue — clean genre / mood / tempo / instrumentation / lyrical-theme tags, with extra care for Apple's heavier metadata weighting.
  • Creative SKILL.md drafted with the layered-depth principle and the four sub-cluster cuts baked in.
  • Spotify for Artists and Apple Music for Artists access confirmed and connected.
What this asks
No paid spend yet. We have no data to optimise against, and burning budget on cold infrastructure teaches us nothing.
Done when
Any future release can fire through the funnel — pre-save live, list captures, Instagram coordinates, metadata is clean, and the creative kit produces.
Phase 02

Calibration release

Run one single through the full cycle at lean budget. The goal is clean baseline data, not a hit.

  • Pre-save submitted ~4 weeks ahead; Apple Music editorial pitch filed in parallel; Tidal Rising / Spotlight submission queued.
  • Four ad sets, one per sub-cluster (sonic neighbours, visual culture, movement, fashion-editorial), with sub-cluster-specific creative cuts from the SKILL.md.
  • Coordinated day-zero push to the list and on Instagram: countdown, drop, a deeper-meaning prompt that runs through the window.
  • List broadcast on release day; the owned surfaces mobilise saves and shares.
  • Window concentration: budget lives in days 0–10, heaviest in days 0–3.
What this asks
Don't pivot mid-window. Resist the urge to "fix" things while it's running. Collect clean data even if the release underperforms — bad data leads to bad decisions for the next ten releases.
Done when
We have a clean read on save rate, CPL by sub-cluster, completion and skip rates, day-one spike size, and early catalogue spillover.
Phase 03

Instrumentation

Wire up the dashboard so we know our actual numbers, not industry averages.

  • Spotify for Artists data + Apple Music for Artists + Meta API into a single view.
  • Compute our real numbers: save rate by sub-cluster, CPL by ad set, skip rate by track second, list growth rate, owned-audience engagement (list, Instagram), catalogue spillover into back-catalogue streams.
  • Identify the sub-cluster that actually converts — not the one we assumed would. The visual-culture or movement clusters might out-perform the sonic-neighbour default, or the other way round. Either is a useful finding.
  • Cross-platform reconciliation: how does an Apple Music "Love" rate compare to a Spotify save? Where do YouTube comments cluster around the signed sections?
What this asks
This is where the data-science side earns the strategy's keep — we replace any Chartmetric price tag and any guesswork with our own observability.
Done when
Every campaign metric lives in one place, and we have Alter Boy's actual benchmarks (not borrowed ones) to optimise against.
Phase 04

Refine

Tune off real data. Make the keep / cut calls.

  • Cull sub-clusters that under-convert; double down on winners. Reallocate budget shares.
  • Identify the creative archetypes that win — specific cuts, hook angles, hand-moment compositions — and codify them back into the SKILL.md so the next release inherits the learning.
  • Decide whether to subscribe to Intellijend (or an equivalent ad-automation SaaS), grounded in: does the time saved at our actual CPL pay back the fee.
  • Calibrate window budget against actual save-rate-to-CPL economics, replacing assumed figures.
  • Lock the release cadence we can sustain creatively and financially — under-promising on cadence is fine; missing one we promised is corrosive.
  • Document an Apple Music editorial pitch template and rhythm now that we know what lands.
  • Revisit Discord: by now we know whether the demand to talk to each other is real. If it is, stand it up properly; if not, leave it.
What this asks
Cut without sentiment — including any audience whose engagement is high but save rate is low. Applause without streams hurts our signal, however nice it feels.
Done when
The engine is tuned, the cadence is set, toolkit decisions are locked in, and the SKILL.md reflects what we now know rather than what we initially guessed.
Phase 05

Steady-state

The ongoing release engine. Every cycle compounds the catalogue, the list and the audience — and feeds the room The Plan is ultimately aiming at.

  • Each release flows through the cycle without us re-thinking it: the SKILL.md produces the creative kit, the dashboard tracks performance, the owned surfaces coordinate day zero, Apple and Tidal get their submissions, the list broadcasts, the algorithm tests us.
  • Catalogue compounds: new Discover Weekly, Apple New Music Mix and YouTube Music Mix listeners get routed into the back-catalogue and captured to the list.
  • Presence deepens: the list and Instagram hold the audience between releases. If a Discord has earned its place by now, it becomes where the most engaged fans live — threads on the layered meanings, live-show coordination, demos. If it hasn't, we don't force it.
  • Catalogue-wide review every few releases: what's the trajectory, what's slipping, what's the next experiment to run alongside (not in place of) the proven loop.
  • Annual recalibration: a fresh look at the benchmarks, the toolkit and the targeting against whatever the platforms have changed in the meantime.
What this asks
Don't break what works. New experiments run alongside the proven loop, not in place of it. Novelty for its own sake corrodes the very compounding the engine exists to produce.
Done when
It doesn't. This is the engine — we're running it now, and handing its output to the back-catalogue and Deepen stages of The Plan.
