For the band · From the depths of HXLL

A plan for getting
the music heard.

We're making good work consistently now. We've never put the same effort into getting it to people. This is what I reckon we do about that, and how it adds up to filling rooms.

Alter Boy · Perth / WA · The Plan, v1
01 — The good news

We've got the hard part down.

For a long time the hard bit was just making the stuff — finishing things, keeping it flowing, staying consistent. That was the struggle for years.

It isn't anymore. We're putting out the best, most consistent work we have.

That part, we've finally sorted.
02 — The bit we skip

But making it was only ever half the job.

We've never been deliberate about marketing or distribution. We make something good, put it out, and hope.

Good work doesn't get heard on its own. It gets heard when someone treats it like a product and takes charge of how it reaches people. We've never really done that.

So I want to take that on.
03 — The landscape

The ways in have changed.

The old ones — triple j rotation, the editorial "fresh find" spots — don't open as easily for a band with a back catalogue as they do for a brand-new act. That's just how it is now.

But there's a newer way in that doesn't care how long we've been around: the algorithm. Discover Weekly, Release Radar, autoplay.

No editor to win over — we just have to earn it.
04 — What we're actually after

It was never really about streams.

Chasing streams for their own sake doesn't add up — nobody makes that money back. What we're actually after is an audience to play our music to.

Mostly the live show, but it can be whatever we want: remixes, side projects, multimodal stuff. That builds our reputation, and reputation is what gets us into the rooms we want.

The Dark Mofo tier
05 — What's in our favour

What we've actually got.

Let's be honest about what we don't have: a big, engaged fanbase. Right now it's more lukewarm awareness and a few industry relationships. But what we do have is harder to come by:

  • A five-year back catalogue to pull from
  • A real track record — big festivals, radio play
  • High-quality creative — live visuals, music videos, photography
  • A strong visual identity, built around Molly
Most acts our size don't have that to work with.

Discover — cast wide, build the flywheel

Grow the listener base with no geographic limits, and optimise purely for what the algorithm actually reads: saves, completion, low early skips, replays. A save is worth far more than a stream — it's the strongest signal we can generate — so we chase saves and cost-per-listener, not raw reach. There's no editor to win over; these playlists put songs in front of people based purely on how listeners behave.

The flywheel
Release (roughly monthly) → front-loaded Meta ads → signal spikes → algorithm picks us up ↻

Each release feeds the next and the catalogue builds up. We borrow these mechanics as they are — the creative stays ours.

How a release runs

Submit a pre-save about four weeks out so we land in followers' Release Radar on day one. Point everything at one smart link, then front-load the ad budget into the first 3–10 days — that early-momentum window is what decides whether the algorithm picks the song up and starts testing it in Discover Weekly and autoplay.

One taste-world, four ways in

We run one audience, split into four sub-clusters at the ad-set level, so we can see which actually converts and shift budget without sentiment. Each gets a creative cut that leads on whatever it cares about most.

Deepen — take the map, fill the room

Discover draws a map of where our listeners actually are — maybe somewhere we wouldn't have guessed. Deepen focuses on wherever that is and asks for more: a ticket, merch, one of Molly's editions.

The live show is the goal, but it's also part of the loop. A sold-out small room gives us footage, word-of-mouth, and the most convincing thing we can show a festival booker. So it's not just spending the audience — it's building the case for the next, bigger room.

07 — The upside

Optimising for streaming discovery can be creatively liberating.

This is the bit that should appeal to us. The old way: sit on songs, batch them into a release, hand it to PR, wait months. The new way lets us make more, faster, and worry less about polish.

  • Put out multiple versions — remixes, slowed, sped up
  • Make short, hook-led cuts built around 15 seconds and a mood
  • Try things and ship them quickly
  • Short and messy beats slow and quarterly
The work that feeds the loop is the same work that lets us experiment.
08 — Where the effort goes

If we only get two things right.

Most of this is plumbing we can rent. Two things we can't — and that's where the effort should go:

01

A creative pipeline built around our world

Turn the live show, the visuals and Molly's world into a steady stream of bespoke cuts. The part nobody else can copy.

02

An audience we own — built, not assumed

We don't have the engaged fanbase yet, so we build it: a list we can email, and a presence that can get a real spike going on day one.

09 — Who we attract

We want listeners, not applause.

Here's the trap. If we lead with what we represent — that we're queer, that we're Deaf and hard-of-hearing, that we sign — we pull in people who admire the cause but never actually press play. That admiration feels good, but it doesn't turn into saves, and saves are the whole game. Worse, it trains the algorithm to file us as a cause instead of a band.

So in the ads and the targeting, we lead with the music and the look — not the identity. People find out who we are once a song's already got their attention. The story is texture that deepens them later, never the hook we use to pull them in.

Work first, story second.
10 — How we get there

Five phases. No deadline.

No 90-day countdown. Each phase ends when it's done, then we move on.

Phase 01
Foundations — stand up the funnel and the list. No ad spend yet.
Phase 02
Calibration release — one single through the whole loop, lean budget. The goal is clean data, not a hit.
Phase 03
Instrumentation — wire up a dashboard so we know our numbers, not industry averages.
Phase 04
Refine — cut what doesn't convert, double down on what does.
Phase 05
Steady-state — the engine just runs, and every release compounds.
11 — Wrapping up

So, the short version.

Cast wide to find people, use the catalogue to bring them closer, concentrate to fill rooms, and build a list we own along the way.

We don't need anyone's permission to do this. We just have to actually do it.

First thing: set up the list, run one release through the whole loop on a small budget to get our own numbers, then decide what's worth scaling. No deadline — each step earns the next.

— Let's give it a crack.
