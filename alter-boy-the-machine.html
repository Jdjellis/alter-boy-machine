The Vision · the short version

Five things that won't change

The strategy is settled. Everything below this rests on these. They're the reason the process looks the way it does.

North star

Saves, not streams

We optimise for saves first, then completion and low early skips. That's the signal the algorithm reads as "people want this."

The fuse

Front-loaded windows

We pour spend into the first 3–10 days of a release, not a constant always-on trickle. A concentrated hit the algorithm can't miss.

The backbone

An audience we own

An email list we control fires a coordinated day-one save spike. Instagram is the day-one rallying surface. We don't rent our launch.

The moat

Creative rooted in our world

We borrow the release mechanics everyone uses — never the faceless creative. Bespoke work grounded in real Alter Boy material is the thing no one can copy.

The frame

Lead with music & look

Never lead with identity. Auslan is choreography and visual signature, not a banner — so the algorithm reads us as a band, not a novelty act.

The net

The back catalogue catches

Five years of material deepens every new listener — turning one-track attention into repeat plays and a real relationship.

The Pipeline · the engine over time

We never run one release. We run a staircase.

At any given week the engine is running several releases at different stages at once — one in its window, one priming, one being cut, a couple slotted behind. 0

Harvest & tag the library

Continuous · not per-release

HVN owns▶

The point

One searchable, tagged clip library so any release can pull the right footage in seconds. This runs in the background all the time — it's the shared foundation every Cut draws from, not a step we repeat per release.

What happens

Source footage lands in one owned master store (never edited, never deleted). It's auto-sliced into shot-level clips, given lightweight playback proxies, then embedded into a semantic index — so we can search it in plain language ("close-up hand moments, dark and slow, signing leads the frame") instead of maintaining a rigid tag vocabulary. The human job collapses to the one thing a machine can't do: marking the standouts.

Who does what

AgentHarvest, shot-detection, proxies, embedding, indexing, answering searches at edit time.
HVNThe taste layer only — burning down the 5-year backlog up front, flagging hero moments a model can't judge, plus a small pass per new shoot. Far smaller ask than tagging every clip.
No gate here — fully internal.

Still open

Which clip-store + index tool we use (decided by a hands-on spike on real footage). And how we handle hard facts semantics can't — aspect ratio, which shoot, and especially clearance/usage rights — so we never ship a public release pulling from un-cleared footage.

1

Slot & set the dial

~4+ weeks out

Publish gate▶

The point

Lock what's coming and how hard we'll push it, and seed the brief everything downstream reads. We plan like a label — penciled, reversible dates on the calendar — but never promise the world a date before the track is real.

What happens

The release is penciled onto the roster at a date that holds the rhythm. The agent drafts a release brief from the master + lyrics (tempo, key, energy, mood, themes, sonic-neighbour artists); a human judges it and adds the angle + who-it's-for. Once the master is locked and delivered with enough lead for the editorial pitch, we commit the date and pre-save opens.

Who does what

AgentMaintains the slot, drafts the brief, preps the distributor submission, runs the master-lock + pitch-lead countdown.
JoshThe gut call on type, judging the brief, and the publish-gate yes.
🔒 Publish gate — committing the track + date to the distributor is irreversible (pre-save gets promised).
2

Curate & cut the kit

~4 to 1 weeks out · the moat

HVN leads QCCreative gate▶

The point

Turn the locked track + the library into the release's creative. This is the moat — where bespoke, grounded work beats the faceless content everyone else runs.

What happens

The agent reads the brief and pulls matching real clips from the library, then assembles a finished draft per cluster to a reusable format recipe — including grounded AI enhancement (animating real stills, building transitions, generating clips anchored to real Alter Boy assets). The hard rule: everything is grounded in our real material and world. Enhancing and generating is fine; untethered generic AI is out.

Who does what

AgentThe assembly machine — retrieval, recipe assembly, grounded enhancement, drafting copy/captions/hooks. Maximum offload.
HVN & JoshConstant QC with full recut authority. This is a real cut-or-recut review, never a rubber stamp — you pick the clips, call the hero moments, judge the grounding, and customise any cluster at will.
🔒 Creative-public gate — a human approves the footage selection and the finished cut before anything can go public.

Still open

How many starting format recipes, and how we keep the QC gate genuinely substantive when 2–3 releases are in flight at once.

3

Prime

~4 to 1 weeks out · overlaps the Cut

Spend · Email · Creative▶

The point

Get everything loaded so day zero is a coordinated hit, not a scramble. By the time the window opens we already know which creative wins.

What happens

The four cluster creatives run as creative angles into broad targeting — we read which creative converts rather than guessing interests up front. A pre-release paid push does double duty: it banks pre-saves (the fuse for the day-one spike) and surfaces the winning creative before the window, so the front-load starts already-optimised. The dial goes hard here, before any money moves. Owned channels get warmed and staged to a one-button day-zero fire.

Who does what

AgentBuilds ad sets, drafts the editorial pitch + emails + IG, schedules everything, monitors the pre-release read, reports.
JoshHolds the gates, confirms the dial, submits the editorial pitch.
🔒 Spend · 🔒 Email · 🔒 Creative-public — every channel that goes live or spends money waits on a human yes.
4

The Window

Days 0–10 · heaviest 0–3

Email · Spend▶

The point

Manufacture the day-0–10 momentum the algorithm reads, and steer the budget by signal. This is the loud part.

What happens

Day zero: the list fires + IG goes; the DSP drop is automatic (we committed it back at Stage 1). We front-load the known winning creative from hour zero. Because saves lag ~1–2 days but Meta is real-time, we steer on two tiers — real-time Meta proxies to cull losers fast, lagged save-rate for the bigger scaling calls. We only escalate spend on releases beating Alter Boy's own rolling baseline, so we never pour money into the winners of a weak release.

Who does what

AgentWatches both signal tiers continuously, reports, and proposes every cull/scale move. It never moves money itself.
JoshFires the day-0 email, approves every spend move at the gate.
🔒 Email (day-zero broadcast) · 🔒 Spend (front-load + every escalation).
5

Spillover & learn

Day 11 → +2 months · the loop-closer

Email▶

The point

Convert the algorithmic pickup into owned audience + catalogue depth, and feed what we learned back into the next release.

What happens

You can't email a Spotify listener, so most attention stays rented inside the DSP. Rather than fight that, we deepen it — routing the one-track listener into the 5-year back catalogue so attention becomes repeat plays — and capture only the keenest into the owned list. No post-window spend. Then we score what won (which clips, recipes, clusters converted); a human ratifies that digest before it biases the next release's curation, with an exploration slice reserved each time so the loop keeps discovering instead of making every release look the same.

Who does what

AgentRoutes, captures, logs, maintains the scores, drafts the nurture sends + the learning digest.
JoshRatifies the digest before it shapes creative; holds the email gate.
🔒 Email — capture/nurture sends only. No spend gate re-enters.

The two-speed read

The fast read (creative ranking, save-rate, baseline) is ready when the window closes → informs the next release. The full read (catalogue depth, rented→owned over two months) lands later → informs the release after that.

How We Plan · the layer above the release

Planned, but agile

The lifecycle governs one release. This layer governs the set of them — a rolling plan the band writes toward, re-planned each cycle as data arrives. Not a fixed calendar; not seat-of-the-pants either. Click to open each.

The roster — what's coming, and when

Rolling-wave planning

▶

Three horizons

Committed: the next 2 releases — song locked, in production, dial set. Firm. Planned: the ~3 beyond — a type + intent ("single, movement-led, targets the dance cluster"), song maybe unfinished; the band writes toward them. Placeholder: a loose backlog of themes and audience gaps, no song attached yet.

Singles anchor, remixes fill

Singles are the anchors — dialled up, they set the editorial-pitch moments and carry the cadence. Remixes/versions are the shock-absorber — chosen reactively to cover the rhythm when no new single's ready, or to reach a cluster recent singles missed. No fixed ratio.

›

Planning drives the songwriting

The differentiator

▶

A reach brief, never a song brief

The plan can name the market-facing frame — target cluster, rough energy/tempo/mood, format and window implications — framed in Alter Boy's own vocabulary ("lean into the Auslan-as-choreography side of who you are"). It never touches creative substance: themes, lyrics, hooks, meaning. It's an invitation with a target, not a directive.

Inspiration has right-of-way

Most releases come from the band's natural flow. We only "write to a slot" when the data proves a gap and the band's up for it — and an empty slot never forces a bad song. If nothing real is ready it converts to a remix or organic content, and the intent rolls forward. The plan biases attention; it never mandates output.

What the machine feeds back

Two distinct layers. Reach learnings tune the roster and the format recipes. Sonic-resonance learnings — what musically resonates in converting tracks — flow back to the band as creative intelligence to inform writing, never as an instruction. The machine surfaces what's working; the band decides if and how it shapes the craft.

›

The board & cadence

Making it visible, holding the rhythm

▶

One flow, left to right

Themes → Planned → Slot → Cut → Prime → Window → Spillover → Archived. A bespoke board (not an off-the-shelf PM tool) where a card is a roster slot. The "hot" columns — Cut and Window — make overload visible: too many cards there at once is the signal to stagger.

Hold the rhythm honestly

The rule on cadence: slip freely before the promise, protect after it. The promise line is when pre-save goes live / we pitch editorial — that's the public commitment. Before it, moving dates is just re-planning. To protect a promised date we'd rather drop the dial (ship a lighter cut or a remix) than break it. And we never dress up a release too thin to clear the signal floor — that's worse than a gap; it wastes the front-load and trains the algorithm wrong.

Where You Fit · HVN

Your two homes in the machine

Almost everything is agent-run. Human craft lands in a few specific, high-value places — and two of them are yours.

Stage 0 · always on

You own the library

The whole machine pulls from one searchable footage library, and you own it. The agent does all the mechanical work — slicing, embedding, indexing. Your job is the taste call a model can't make: burning down the 5-year backlog up front and flagging the hero moments. Populating the library is the gate on starting the mechanism for real.

Stage 2 · every release

You lead QC on the cut

The agent assembles a finished draft per cluster; you and Josh are the constant QC layer with full recut authority. You pick the clips, call the hero moments, judge whether the AI work is properly grounded in our world, and customise any cluster. This is the moat — protected by your taste and the gate, not by limiting what the agent can reach for.

Why it's a smaller ask than the old plan: the old approach wanted three people correcting machine tags on every single clip. Semantic search killed that. You're left with only the judgments that genuinely need a human eye.
